Chapter I

A Life Before Leaving

School in Algeria is serious business. From the earliest years, there is an understanding — unspoken but present in every classroom — that education is the primary path forward.

I grew up inside that system, in Annaba, in the northeast of the country. Annaba is not a small city — it has history, industry, a university of its own, a port that has been active for centuries.

Chapter I — continued

A Life Before Leaving

My school years were shaped by the Algerian curriculum — dense, demanding, and weighted heavily toward mathematics and the sciences. The baccalaureate loomed over the final years of lycée the way a storm looms on the horizon: you could see it coming long before it arrived, and everything in the meantime was preparation.

What the Algerian school system gave me — and I say this without irony — was discipline. The ability to sit with difficult material until it became familiar. The habit of working when motivation is absent. A baseline respect for rigour that I would later come to appreciate fully only when I encountered environments where it was also expected of me.

Chapter II

The Departure

There is a particular kind of silence that fills a home the night before someone leaves it for a long time. In Annaba, that silence is thick with sea air and the distant sound of the Mediterranean.

I was nineteen years old, and in a few hours I would be on a plane to Qatar. Leaving felt like two things at once: pride and difficulty, wrapped so tightly around each other that I could not tell where one ended and the other began. My father shook my hand at the airport the way he does when he means something more than words allow.

Chapter III

Arriving in Doha

The first thing Doha does is overwhelm you with scale. Coming from Annaba — a city of warmth and history, of narrow streets and colonial architecture leaning into the sea — the skyline of Doha reads almost like a statement.

Glass towers rising out of flat desert. Highways wide enough to feel like declarations. Everything new, everything deliberate. The heat pressed against me like something physical. I remember thinking: this place does not accommodate. You adjust to it.

Chapter IV

The Academic Journey

Supply Chain Management is not a subject that announces itself dramatically. But it is deeply practical, and I had chosen it precisely for that reason — to understand how things move across complex systems. In Qatar, a country that imports the vast majority of what it consumes, that question felt immediately relevant.

My classmates came from across the world — from Asia, Africa, the Middle East, Europe. Working alongside people with genuinely different educational backgrounds taught me things no textbook covers.

Chapter V

Living Between Two Worlds

At some point during the first semester, I stopped counting the days. Not because I had stopped missing home, but because the missing had become something I carried rather than something that stopped me. Annaba remained vivid — the sound of the port, the smell of the medina in summer, the particular quality of light over the bay in the late afternoon.

Being Algerian in Qatar places you in a particular position. You are Arab, but differently Arab. I found myself explaining Algeria constantly, and in doing so, understanding it more clearly than I had when I lived inside it.

Chapter VI

What Qatar Taught Me

Qatar is a country that has made a conscious decision about what it wants to become. That ambition is visible in its infrastructure, its institutions, and the deliberate investment in education that brought universities like Lusail into existence.

I arrived from a country with enormous potential and significant friction. In Qatar, I saw what it looks like when systems are prioritised. It did not make me disillusioned with home. It made me more interested in it.

Chapter VII

Looking Forward

I am still studying. Still adjusting. Still learning, semester by semester, what it means to build a life across two places at once. There are days in Doha when I miss the sea in Annaba so much it is almost physical. There are days back home when I feel the pull of everything I left in Qatar.

I left Annaba at nineteen to study Supply Chain Management at Lusail University. What I did not expect was that the most important supply chain I would study would be the one connecting who I was to who I am becoming — each link formed by a flight, a semester, a conversation, a challenge met or failed and tried again.

That chain is still being built. I am still in the middle of it.
